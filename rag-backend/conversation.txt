1. Generating many different ideas
2. Exploring various possibilities
3. No constraints or judgments on the ideas produced
4. Accessing memory banks to create novel combinations
5. Suppressing autobiographical narratives and past judgments

Convergent thinking, on the other hand, is the process of narrowing down ideas to arrive at a specific solution or creation. It involves:

1. Focusing on finding the correct or best answer
2. Combining different elements in a meaningful way
3. Requiring more focus and persistence
4. Often feeling more challenging than divergent thinking
5. Relying on distinct brain circuits compared to divergent thinking

An example of convergent thinking would be combining the concepts of "wing," "water," and "engine" to arrive at the idea of a plane that can land on water.

Both divergent and convergent thinking are essential for creativity. The creative process typically involves first engaging in divergent thinking to generate ideas, followed by convergent thinking to refine and actualize those ideas into a specific creation or solution.

It's important to note that while everyone can improve their divergent and convergent thinking skills through practice, the ability to do so may decrease with age. However, both skills are necessary for creativity, regardless of one's natural inclination towards one or the other.
